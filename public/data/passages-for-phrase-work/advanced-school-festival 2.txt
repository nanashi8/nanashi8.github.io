It was ambitious,

but we were excited

about the challenge.

"All right,

let's form committees," the president said.

"We need groups for drama, food,

decorations, and publicity."

I joined the drama committee along with ten other students.

Our first task was choosing a script.

"That's a good idea," our drama advisor, Ms.

Tanaka, agreed.

We decided on a retelling of Cinderella,

I tried out for the role of the narrator.

When it was my turn,

I stood on the small stage in our classroom.

"Take a deep breath," Ms.

Tanaka encouraged.

"Just do your best.

We're not looking for perfection—we want

to see your personality."

I read my lines, trying not to shake.

When I finished, everyone clapped politely.

A few days later, Ms.

Tanaka announced the cast.

"Congratulations to everyone!

Remember, there are no small parts—every role is important for our success."

I got the narrator role!

I was thrilled but also nervous

"We'll do well

The class agreed enthusiastically.

We held a design contest,

and everyone submitted ideas.

The winning design featured our class number (2-B) with comedy and tragedy masks and the words "Drama and Dreams" in both English and Japanese.

"These T-shirts are going

to be amazing!" Aiko beamed.

"

When you wear them on festival day,

Two weeks later, the T-shirts arrived.

They looked professional and cool.

We tried them on immediately.

"Perfect fit!" said Yumi,

"Make sure

The decoration committee faced a big challenge: creating a convincing set with almost no budget.

the decoration leader.

"We need a castle backdrop, furniture,

and props,

"Let's ask other classes and teachers

"And we could make some things ourselves from cardboard and paint."

The art teacher lent us paint and brushes.

The previous year's drama class donated old props.

"Look at all this stuff!" Hiroshi said,

surveying our collected materials.

"We might actually pull this off!"

Construction began during lunch breaks and after school.

Students who weren't good at acting discovered they were talented at building and painting.

The castle backdrop slowly took shape,

painted in shades of gray and purple

"I'm so sorry!

I ruined everything!"

"Wait—what if we make it look intentional?

We quickly adjusted the design,

adding more blue streaks and silver sparkles.

The "accident" actually made the backdrop more interesting and dynamic.

"See?" Hiroshi laughed.

"Even mistakes can turn into something good

if you're creative!"

The food committee decided

to sell onigiri (rice balls),

cookies, and juice.

"These are easy

to prepare and won't require cooking equipment," explained the committee leader, Sakura.

But they faced their own challenge: none of them had experience making food for large groups.

"My mom knows how," offered Mei.

"She used to work at a restaurant.

I'll ask if she can teach us."

Mei's mother kindly agreed

to give a cooking lesson.

One Saturday,

fifteen students gathered at Mei's house

to learn proper onigiri-making techniques.

"The key is the right amount of rice and filling," Mei's mother explained, demonstrating.

"Too much, and it falls apart.

Too little, and it's boring.

Here—everyone try."

We practiced making dozens of onigiri.

Some were misshapen lumps,

others fell apart,

but gradually we improved.

"These aren't perfect,

but they taste good!" Sakura said,

biting into one.

"With more practice, we'll be ready."

As the festival approached, rehearsals intensified.

We practiced every day

after school, sometimes

until 6 PM.

"Let's run

through the quick-change scene again," Ms.

Tanaka called.

"The transformation from rags

to ball gown needs

to happen in under thirty seconds."

Yumi, playing Cinderella, had

to take off her "poor" costume and put on her fancy dress

We'd designed a special costume with velcro and magnetic closures for speed.

"Ready...

Tanaka clicked her stopwatch.

Yumi disappeared behind the screen.

We heard fumbling and whispered curses.

"Forty-five seconds," Ms.

Tanaka announced.

"Still too slow.

The audience will get bored."

We practiced the quick change over and over.

Yumi got faster,

and we added music

to cover the transition time.

Finally, we got it down

to twenty-eight seconds.

"Perfect!" Ms.

Tanaka smiled.

"That's the kind of problem-solving I like

to see.

You identified a problem and worked together

Two days

before the festival,

we held a full dress rehearsal—and everything went wrong.

The curtain got stuck.

Someone forgot their lines.

A prop sword broke.

The sound system malfunctioned.

Kenji tripped during the dance scene.

After rehearsal, everyone looked discouraged.

"That was terrible," Kenji groaned.

"We're going

We stared at her in disbelief.

"There's a theater saying: bad dress rehearsal,

good opening night.

All these problems happened now,

when we can fix them.

Better now than during the actual performance.

Let's make a list and solve each issue one by one."

We stayed late making repairs and adjustments.

The curtain mechanism was fixed.

Everyone reviewed their lines.

We made a backup sword from sturdier materials.

The AV club helped troubleshoot the sound system.

"Remember," Ms.

Tanaka said

as we finally left

that night,

"you're all doing wonderfully.

Small problems don't mean you'll fail.

They mean you're learning."

The morning of the school festival,

I woke up excited and terrified.

I put on my class T-shirt with pride,

feeling like part of something special.

At school, the campus buzzed with energy.

Every class was setting up their projects.

Colorful decorations hung everywhere.

In the morning,

we helped run the onigiri stand.

Fresh rice balls!

"We should have made more!" Sakura laughed,

"This will definitely help fund next year's festival activities."

Everyone wore their T-shirts and looked professional.

The nervousness was palpable.

"Circle up," Ms.

Tanaka called.

We formed a huddle,

arms around each other's shoulders.

"You've worked so hard," she said emotionally.

"I'm proud of every single one of you—not just for this play,

but for how you've grown

as a team.

No matter what happens out there,

you've already succeeded.

"Class 2-B!" we shouted together, pumping our fists.

The auditorium was packed—at least two hundred people.

My heart pounded

as I stepped onto the stage

to begin my narration.

"Once upon a time,

in a school much like this one,

to the festival..."

The audience quieted, listening.

I relaxed slightly.

We can do this, I thought.

The performance went amazingly well.

Yes, one person stumbled over a line,

and a prop fell over unexpectedly,

but we recovered quickly.

The audience laughed at the funny parts and applauded

after each scene.

Yumi's quick costume change worked perfectly.

Kenji's dramatic prince entrance got cheers.

The modern updates

to the classic story—the "glass slipper" became a lost student ID,

the "fairy godmother" was a helpful school librarian—delighted everyone.

For the finale,

all of us came on stage together.

The audience gave us a standing ovation.

I felt tears in my eyes

as I bowed with my classmates.

"Drama crew, carefully pack all props and costumes.

Decoration team,

Through this experience,

Yumi had discovered she loved performing.

Hiroshi had found his talent for organization and construction.

Kenji had learned

None of us could have created this project alone,

Planning and preparation matter.

design work,

Reflecting years later,

I recognized

that the school festival exemplified progressive educational philosophy emphasizing experiential learning over passive instruction.

Rather than merely absorbing information from textbooks and lectures,

we actively created something,

solving authentic problems requiring collaboration, creativity,

and critical thinking.

Traditional education often focuses narrowly on academic subjects—mathematics,

science, literature, history.

These disciplines matter enormously,

providing foundational knowledge essential for informed citizenship and professional competence.

However, they constitute only one dimension of education.

Project-based learning,

exemplified by festival preparation,

develops complementary capacities: communication, teamwork, leadership,

problem-solving,

time management,

emotional regulation, resilience.

These "soft skills" (

though the term understates their importance) prove equally vital for personal and professional success.

Research consistently demonstrates

that people learn more effectively

through active engagement than passive reception.

We remember experiences more vividly than lectures.

Skills developed

through practice become internalized more thoroughly than abstract concepts studied theoretically.

Moreover, collaborative projects cultivate social-emotional competencies increasingly recognized

as crucial.

Understanding others' perspectives,

negotiating conflicts constructively,

maintaining composure under pressure,

persisting despite setbacks—these capacities determine success

as much

as intellectual aptitude.

The festival also illustrated how arbitrary boundaries

between academic disciplines dissolve in real-world contexts.

Our project required mathematical calculations (budgeting),

scientific principles (lighting, sound),

linguistic skills (scriptwriting, translation),

historical knowledge (cultural references),

artistic creativity (set design, costumes),

technological proficiency (video editing, sound equipment).

Authentic challenges demand integrated knowledge rather than siloed expertise.

Educational systems worldwide are gradually recognizing these principles,

incorporating more experiential,

project-based, interdisciplinary approaches.

However, traditional models emphasizing standardized testing and passive learning persist, partly

because they're easier

to administer and assess.

Yet the evidence overwhelmingly supports more active, engaged,

collaborative learning environments.

Students taught

through such methods demonstrate superior retention,

deeper understanding,

greater motivation,

and better transfer of knowledge

to novel situations.

The school festival wasn't peripheral

to education—it was education at its finest.

We learned lessons

that transcended any single subject,

developing capabilities

that would serve us

throughout life in ways

that classroom instruction alone could never achieve.
